Project #1

JJ Dupcak
Sept 10 2025

I printed this edited multicolored nametag utilizing the PrusaMINI+ FDM printer, with light gray and bright green PLA filament. After downloading the editable premade 3MF file and importing it directly into the PrusaSlicer software, I was able to change the placeholder text to my name, change the font, and bold the text. Since this model was a two color design, I read through the related section in the PrusaMINI+ manual and the PrusaSlicer manual. As the 3MF file already contained a color change indication built into the model, I double checked to make sure it would be included in the final G-Code. After slicing and exporting the G-Code to a USB, I printed my custom nametag on the PrusaMINI+.


My torture test print went relatively well apart from some extraneous wisps and small lines of PLA in the open portions of the structure. The measurements seem to be quite accurate, as each bar in the lattice is supposed to be around 2mm, and measuring with a cm ruler I estimated them to be 2mm. However, as this is a well designed model, it prevents failure from pushing the limitations of the 3D printer too far. The lattice structure is specifically set up to support itself, however, gravity and overhang can cause a print to fail or distort. More design limitations include printable size, shrinkage, resolution of the printer (inability to print sharp details or perfectly straight corners). A 3MF (3D Manufacturing) file contains a lot more information than a regular STL file, which only contains mesh data consisting of triangles. A 3MF can contain color data, material, licensing data, and even printing settings for the slicer.


I printed a medium sized bag carrier, which works very well in distributing the weight of one or multiple shopping bags. I did not have several shopping bags to test it with, but a fully packed small plastic bag worked well with the device. This model was created by the Neil Squire Society for people who suffer from arthritis or other conditions in which hand movement and comfort are affected, as it helps the user distribute the weight and get a stronger, stable grip while carrying bags. The documentation was interesting to see, as it included the build files, and detailed writeups regarding the purpose of the device’s use and pictures of the first iteration, which was tweaked to become the model I have printed. A limitation that could be worked around is the potential problem of bags loops sliding off if the user tilts it at certain angles when moving around/picking them up, by making the “hook” portion more complete, however, this runs the risk of making it more difficult for those with less finger dexterity to be able to thread the loops of a bag on. Perhaps a unidirectional spring mechanism that acts as a gate to close off the loop would prevent this, but would also make the device far less accessible and simple in terms of printing/creating.
