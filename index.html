Project #1

JJ Dupcak
Sept 10 2025

I printed this edited multicolored nametag utilizing the PrusaMINI+ FDM printer, with light gray and bright green PLA filament. After downloading the editable premade 3MF file and importing it directly into the PrusaSlicer software, I was able to change the placeholder text to my name, change the font, and bold the text. Since this model was a two color design, I read through the related section in the PrusaMINI+ manual and the PrusaSlicer manual. As the 3MF file already contained a color change indication built into the model, I double checked to make sure it would be included in the final G-Code. After slicing and exporting the G-Code to a USB, I printed my custom nametag on the PrusaMINI+.


My torture test print went relatively well apart from some extraneous wisps and small lines of PLA in the open portions of the structure. The measurements seem to be quite accurate, as each bar in the lattice is supposed to be around 2mm, and measuring with a cm ruler I estimated them to be 2mm. However, as this is a well designed model, it prevents failure from pushing the limitations of the 3D printer too far. The lattice structure is specifically set up to support itself, however, gravity and overhang can cause a print to fail or distort. More design limitations include printable size, shrinkage, resolution of the printer (inability to print sharp details or perfectly straight corners). A 3MF (3D Manufacturing) file contains a lot more information than a regular STL file, which only contains mesh data consisting of triangles. A 3MF can contain color data, material, licensing data, and even printing settings for the slicer.


I printed a medium sized bag carrier, which works very well in distributing the weight of one or multiple shopping bags. I did not have several shopping bags to test it with, but a fully packed small plastic bag worked well with the device. This model was created by the Neil Squire Society for people who suffer from arthritis or other conditions in which hand movement and comfort are affected, as it helps the user distribute the weight and get a stronger, stable grip while carrying bags. The documentation was interesting to see, as it included the build files, and detailed writeups regarding the purpose of the device’s use and pictures of the first iteration, which was tweaked to become the model I have printed. A limitation that could be worked around is the potential problem of bags loops sliding off if the user tilts it at certain angles when moving around/picking them up, by making the “hook” portion more complete, however, this runs the risk of making it more difficult for those with less finger dexterity to be able to thread the loops of a bag on. Perhaps a unidirectional spring mechanism that acts as a gate to close off the loop would prevent this, but would also make the device far less accessible and simple in terms of printing/creating.

Project #2

JJ Dupcak
Sept 22 2025

For part one of this project, TinkerCAD felt pretty limited, and almost harder to work than Fusion, as I am used to a workflow more akin to Fusion than TinkerCAD. Thankfully I do have a good amount of experience in thinking and working in curve/sketch based CAD, but with Rhino3D rather than Fusion. The UI and workflow is definitely a bit different, but I do like the fact that it is highly parametric and works pretty smoothly. In TinkerCAD, I created this abstract helix knot thing. I used Fusion to create a similar helix, with the same amount of rotation, direction, thickness, etc. and manipulated and mirrored it in the same way that I had done with TinkerCAD.



For part two of the project, I searched through functional prints online, eventually finding a no drill/no tool clamp that can be used for the school issued desk in my dorm. I have some speaker monitors that sit on top of the upper shelf of the desk. The left speaker has the power cable output, which, when plugged into the power strip on the floor, hangs annoyingly behind the middle of the desk, and isn’t long enough to be draped over the top edge of the desk. Taping it to the very back of the desk only lasts a couple days with scotch tape. So, I designed and drew out changes I could make to the clamp I found, according to the measurements of the desk. I added a hook, and bar to keep the cable in place/guiding it down to the outlet and preventing it from sticking out in any way.


My desk with the hanging speaker cord.

Very messy sketch to plan things out.

The clamp as found on the internet.

Simple TinkerCAD edits. Some dimensions still need to be edited.

The TinkerCad model was a very rudimentary “sketch” of the design, as I needed to change dimensions without changing the boolean cut out of the threads that were included in the design, and TinkerCad does not allow for complex changes like that.


Clamp after Fusion360 edits.

In Fusion, I turned the STL into a solid body using face groups. The threads in the body of the clamp converted cleanly. Unfortunately, I could not convert the bolt to a solid body, as it was breaking the surfaces, warping them etc., and this would lead to the bolt not fitting and threading properly. I edited the design a bit to reduce overhang. To print, I used the .20mm (Speed) setting, with a 30% infill.


Design in use.

Successfully clears the speaker cord from view while sitting at my desk.

The final print came out nicely, with only a bit of warping on the chamfered edges, some small cracking and small holes in the threads of the bolt. The threading mechanism works very well, however, the bolt does wiggle around slightly, and in a future version I could find out if this is due to a printing error, ie. warping, or due to the tolerance value on the female tap being too large. It would probably only need around 0.05 - 0.075mm less tolerance from its current 0.3mm. I would also make the elements I added a couple mm thicker, and increase the fill of the entire design, as I could see/hear some bending when tightening the bolt onto the desk. I would also remove the bar that keeps the cable in place, as I originally had thought the wire would have more “spring” in it that would cause it to stick outwards away from the wall, but this ended up not being the case, and the bar is unnecessary. Overall however, it does a great job of keeping the cord out of view while still being able to reach the power strip.

Project #3

JJ Dupcak
Oct 27 2025

For this project, I chose the large radio to solder and build an enclosure for. After reading the instruction manual, I knew it would be difficult to make sure each part went where it was supposed to, and was soldered on correctly. One thing I would absolutely change is the format, including more pictures, and rearranging the instructions so that it would be step by step, rather than a small checklist, and then very large expanded instructions for only a couple of the components. However, in the instructions it did provide, they were mostly clear and educational.



The soldering process turned out to be difficult, due to switching irons, and most annoyingly, the solder not flowing, regardless of how hot the piece was. Ziggy and I began on one radio, but as time ran out and he had other commitments, we eventually just worked on two soldering stations to try and get the work done.


I then took measurements of any important dimension that would help me in designing and sketching my enclosure in CAD. To assist with the sketching, I used a planar schematic photo from an online upload of the manual. I scaled the photo, double checked with my measurement references and sketches, and began creating the planar curves for each side of the radio. The affordances that I needed to allow for were the changing and access to the battery, a hole for the knob, buttons, speakers, antenna, and eventually adding a hole for the LED light.

For the first print to save time, I printed just the front face. Only some minor adjustments had to be made in terms of tolerance, as I wanted to get the button hole a bit smaller, and move the hole for the speaker by a mm or two. I also decided to add a hole for the LED light to shine through, as this is an important signifier of the functioning radio. I designed an enclosure with a sliding mechanism that locks itself in using normal force, printed in three parts, with 0.2mm tolerance.


Final 3D printed enclosure design with sliding mechanism.

Print settings for the enclosure.

Then, I researched how to create a finger joint mechanism for my laser cut piece. I created it to scale using 3mm thickness parts, editing my previous design and altering dimensions to work with the new mechanism. Unfortunately, since I only had one class to laser cut, I was not entirely happy with the outcome. I had inadvertently made the enclosure too deep, which caused the circuit board to be loose within the enclosure. Also, if I had more time, I would have used lightburn to make the final outer dimension cuts last rather than midway through as the pieces dropped midway, messing up the dimensions for some of the holes, causing its measurements to be nearly 5mm off of the actual design. However, I am very happy with how the finger joints came out, which worked neatly. For a more sturdy fit, I would put some wood glue in between the joints to lock it in place.

Project #4

JJ Dupcak
Nov 3 2025

My project can be found here.



For this project, I unfortunately had a late start with only one in class working day, but I’m satisfied with how it came out, if I had more time for iterations, I would have come up with a way to allow for more shapes and sizes of screwdrivers to fit, while still allowing each screwdriver to fit nicely in its container. Since the drawer with screwdrivers in the front of the class is not yet organized, I decided to create organizer cases, based on and working with the Gridfinity system. The system works with a simple printed grid, beveled and cut into to easily support any modular additions, objects, organizers, etc.


Since the grid is often printed in 42 x 42mm inner dimension grid sizes, and the grid that has been printed in class has these dimensions, I chose to work with those. On the Gridfinity website, there are clear and precise instructions on how to create compatible, modular models that slot in. I followed these instructions, added holes for M3 screws, which some users of the Gridfinity system use, and created the initial “bin” for the screwdrivers.

The Gridfinity system specifications.

The grid I created from these specifications to add to my model.

In order to create the correct sizing and good cutouts to fit the screwdrivers well, I measured all dimensions, took pictures, and modeled the silhouettes in a sketch. Then, using the depth measurements I took, I modeled a similar contoured handle and volume, allowing for looser curves and greater tolerance for the sake of compatibility with other screwdrivers. I then added a cutout lip that matches the dimensions and angles of the pieces' bottom grids, to allow for stacking while not storing screwdrivers. I had thought about adding enough depth to be able to stack them while in use, however the models would have ended up too tall and clunky, and greatly increase printing time. Lastly, I added cutouts on each end to make it easier to take each screwdriver out of its organizer.


Measurements and early design notes.

Final medium sized design.

All three sizes.

Here is the documentation + description that is included in my model post on the Thangs website, where many other Gridfinity models can be found:

Three sized screwdriver holders made for Wiha 53XX "XHeavy Duty" screwdriver sets, fully compatible with the Gridfinity system. Any comparably sized screwdrivers will fit, but specifically made for the small, medium and large sized handles in the Wiha set. No supports needed for printing, however build size is over 300mm for the large size.

Screwdrivers are sized as the following from tip of metal to bottom of handle, and handle width:

-Small: 187mm x 30mm (#1x80mm Phillips).

-Medium: 240mm x 35mm (#2x100mm Phillips, 5.5mmx100mm Flathead, 6.5x125mm Flathead).

-Large: 280mm x 40mm (8.0mm x 150mm Flathead).

Cut outs on ends allow for easier removal from units. Units are stackable when not in use.

Grid measurements, angles, tolerance, and M3 screw holes are in compliance with the Gridfinity technical specifications. See: Gridfinity for more info.

Added tolerance may allow for other brands and lengths of screwdrivers & handles with differing curvatures to fit.
